## Thesis

The acqui-hire has become the default M&A playbook for AI talent acquisition. The premise is simple: buying a team is faster than recruiting one. But the data tells a different story.

Across 47 AI acqui-hires completed between 2021 and 2024, the median retention rate at 18 months post-close is just 40%. This means buyers are paying talent premiums for teams that are, on average, less than half intact before the first earnout milestone.

The Retention Half-Life Index provides a framework for quantifying this risk.

## The Retention Half-Life Index

Borrowing from nuclear physics, we model talent retention as exponential decay:

**N(t) = N₀ × e^(-λt)**

Where N₀ is the acquired headcount, t is months post-close, and λ (lambda) is the decay constant derived from:

- **Compensation delta**: Gap between acquirer's total comp and market rate for equivalent AI roles
- **Autonomy index**: Degree of product independence promised vs. delivered
- **Cultural distance**: Measured by org size ratio, decision-making speed, and tech stack overlap
- **Geographic pull**: Proximity to alternative employers (SF Bay Area = highest λ)

The resulting half-life (t½ = ln(2)/λ) gives a single number that predicts when half the acquired team will have departed.

## Key Findings

**Compensation is necessary but not sufficient.** Acquirers that matched or exceeded market compensation saw only a 15% improvement in retention. The dominant factor is autonomy — teams that maintained independent repos and deployment authority retained 2.1x better than those integrated into parent engineering orgs.

**The 12-month cliff is real.** The steepest retention drop occurs between months 10 and 14, coinciding with the typical first equity vesting event and the point at which integration promises are tested against reality.

**Geography matters asymmetrically.** SF-based acqui-hires have a half-life of 11 months versus 19 months for those in markets with fewer alternative employers. Remote-first acquirers see the worst retention of all (9 months) — suggesting that physical co-location creates friction that paradoxically improves retention.

## Recommendation

For buyers: model retention decay into your acquisition price using the Retention Half-Life Index. A 14-month half-life means your $200M acqui-hire is effectively a $100M acqui-hire by month 14. Price accordingly.

For sellers: the strongest negotiating position is one that demonstrates your team has low λ — emphasize cultural distinctiveness, geographic concentration, and the irreplaceability of team-specific knowledge.
